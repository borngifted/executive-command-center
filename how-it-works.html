The problem it solves

Independent music companies lose money in quiet, boring ways: a song goes out before the contracts are signed, ownership shares that don't add up, a royalty statement nobody double-checked. None of it looks like a crisis on the day it happens — it becomes one months later.

This dashboard makes those problems impossible to miss. It reads everything in the catalog and puts a plain warning at the top of the screen while there's still time to fix it.

The five screens, in plain English

Screen 1

Overview — the car dashboard

Like the dashboard in your car: speed, fuel, and warning lights. You see how many artists and releases you have, how much revenue came in, and — most importantly — a list of warning lights.

What a warning looks like
high  "Midnight EP" — Missing ISRC: "Midnight"

Translation: one song is missing its serial number, and without it the release can't be distributed. Click the warning and it takes you straight to the problem.

Screen 2

Approvals — nothing ships without a signature

Think of signing checks. Every release stops at your desk before it goes out, with a simple checklist: ✓ Metadata ✓ Assets ✗ Contracts — green means done, red means missing.

You can still approve something with an open issue — you're the executive — but the system makes you confirm it a second time, and it writes the decision down. Which brings us to the receipt:

The audit log — every decision gets a receipt
"A. Reeves (executive): Approved 'First Light — Single' WITH 1 open risk(s)"

Six months later, nobody argues about who approved what. It's written down, with a date and a name.

Screen 3

Artists & Rights — the deed box

For every song: who owns it, who gets paid, and whether the paperwork is actually on file.

Splits are like splitting a restaurant bill. If four people split a bill, the shares must add up to exactly 100%. If they only add up to 92%, someone is going to be unhappy — and in music, that someone sues. The dashboard adds up every song's shares and turns the row red if they don't hit 100%:

Owning masters vs. publishing is like owning a house vs. renting. If the artist owns the recording (the "master") and the songwriting (the "publishing"), they own the house. If a label or a third party owns them, the artist is a tenant in their own song. Every song gets a simple protection grade, A through F — an A means the house, the deed, and the paperwork are all in the artist's name.

Screen 4

Numbers Check — the bank statement that has to add up

Reported numbers get checked the way you'd check a bank statement. Two simple tests:

  • Is the math possible? You cannot sell 61,000 copies of a record if you only shipped 50,000. If a report says that, the report is wrong — and the dashboard stamps it Impossible math.
  • Are the fans real? Five million streams sounds wonderful. But if only 360 people actually bought anything, that's applause, not customers. The dashboard shows the honest conversion rate next to the big flashy number.
Example from the demo

"Fast Money" reports 5,100,000 streams — and 360 purchases. Verified sales: 61,000. Units shipped: 50,000. Verdict: Impossible math. Somebody's numbers are cooked, and now you know before you spend against them.

Screen 5

Trends & Radio — are we actually growing?

A simple line: streams per month, going up or not. A funnel showing how many listeners became savers, and how many savers became buyers. And a radio board showing where songs got played — with every report labeled by where it came from and how it was verified, so nobody passes off a rumor as a chart.

Built-in safeguards

  • Roles. A manager can see the pipeline but cannot approve a release — the buttons simply aren't there for them. Only executives and admins can sign off. There's a "Viewing as" switcher in the demo so you can see this for yourself.
  • The audit log. Every approval and rejection is recorded with who, what, and when. Like a receipt drawer that fills itself.
  • Warnings before signatures. The system never blocks the boss — but it never lets a risk hide, either. Approving past a warning is allowed, visible, and logged.

Where this stands today

What you're looking at is a working prototype. Everything on screen works — the approvals, the warnings, the math checks — but it runs on realistic sample data, on purpose. The sample catalog is deliberately seeded with problems (a 92% split, a missing serial number, a cooked sales report) so every feature demonstrates itself in the demo.

The next step is connecting it to a real catalog: real uploads, real logins, real royalty data. The screens and the logic you see are exactly what that production version will use.

Small glossary

ISRC
A song's serial number. Every legitimate recording has one; distributors require it.
UPC
The barcode for a whole release (a single, EP, or album).
Splits
Who gets what percentage of a song's money. Must always total exactly 100%.
Master
Ownership of the actual recording. Whoever owns the master controls the money the recording earns.
Publishing
Ownership of the songwriting — the words and melody — separate from the recording.
Split sheet
The signed piece of paper where everyone agrees on the percentages. No sheet, no proof.
Spin
One play of a song on a radio station or show.
